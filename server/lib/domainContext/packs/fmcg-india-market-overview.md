This pack frames how the Indian FMCG market behaves, so analytical answers can be interpreted in the right operating context. All claims are qualitative; quantitative magnitudes must come from the dataset, not from this pack.

## Market structure

- The Indian FMCG market is one of the largest in the world by volume but middle-tier by value, reflecting low ticket sizes and high frequency of consumption.
- Categories are dominated by **a small number of large branded players** (HUL, ITC, Nestlé, Marico, Dabur, Britannia, Godrej Consumer, Colgate-Palmolive, P&G, Reckitt) plus regional and unbranded competition that varies sharply by state.
- The **sachet economy** — small unit packs at low absolute price — is a defining feature; it widens distribution and lets brands reach low-income consumers without dropping unit margin to unsustainable levels.
- **Penetration vs. consumption** is a key analytical distinction: many categories have already achieved high penetration in urban India, so growth is now driven by upgrade (premiumisation) and frequency rather than first-time adoption.

## Urban vs. rural

- Roughly two-thirds of the population is rural; rural FMCG demand is more sensitive to monsoon, agricultural income, MNREGA spend and election cycles than to urban macros.
- Urban demand is more sensitive to inflation in food and fuel, real-estate cycles, and white-collar hiring sentiment.
- Rural recovery / weakness is one of the most-discussed macro themes in the FMCG sector and shows up in management commentary every quarter.
- The urban/rural split is a meaningful secondary dimension for almost any volume question.

## Growth dynamics

- **Volume growth** in mature categories typically runs in the low-to-mid single digits over long horizons; **value growth** can diverge sharply during periods of input-cost inflation as price pass-through outpaces volume.
- **Premiumisation** — consumers trading up to higher-priced variants within a category — is a structural tailwind for most large players, with quick commerce and e-commerce as the enabling channels.
- **D2C and quick commerce** are the fastest-growing channels; modern trade is steady; general trade is the largest in absolute terms but slowest growing.
- **Inflation** changes the value/volume mix sharply: high inflation tends to compress volume growth (consumers downtrade to smaller packs) while inflating value growth (price pass-through).
- A "value vs. volume" gap in the dataset usually has a pricing-pass-through explanation, not a demand explanation.

## Patterns to keep in mind

- "Growth" in FMCG analysis should always be qualified as value or volume — they routinely diverge.
- Channel mix shifts can swamp underlying brand performance; always check the channel cut before attributing a movement to brand action.
- Seasonality is significant for many categories (see seasonality pack); year-on-year (YoY) is almost always the right comparison, not month-on-month.
